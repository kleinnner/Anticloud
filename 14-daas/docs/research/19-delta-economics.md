# We defined the economics of superposition computing — price, cost, and value.

**Δ Economics: The Price of Superposition and the Cost of Collapse**

---

## The Problem

The cloud pricing model is not designed to reflect the value of computing. It is designed to capture ongoing revenue. Infrastructure pricing is opaque (compute, storage, egress, API calls, support tiers), subscription pricing is extractive (you pay forever for code that never changes), and both models assume that the provider is entitled to a share of every value the customer creates. There is no alternative economic model that aligns the vendor's incentive with the customer's sovereignty — because the entire industry is built on the assumption that computing is a recurring expense, not a capital asset.

## What We Built

We defined the Δ economic model: computing as a capital asset, not an operating expense. Under the Δ model, the binary is the product — it is purchased once and owned permanently. The price reflects the value of the capability, not the cost of hosting it. Updates, support, and additional services are sold separately and are optional. The binary never expires, never phones home, and never requires ongoing payment to function. The economics are transparent: one price, clearly stated, with no hidden recurring costs. The customer knows exactly what they are buying and exactly what it will cost over the lifetime of the product.

## The Research

We present the Δ economic model for software: one-time purchase of a self-contained binary with no runtime dependencies on the vendor. We analyze the total cost of ownership over a 5-year horizon and demonstrate that the Δ model is significantly cheaper than equivalent SaaS subscriptions, even at premium one-time prices. The analysis accounts for: hosting costs eliminated (the customer's existing hardware suffices), subscription costs eliminated (no monthly payments), migration costs eliminated (no vendor lock-in), and upgrade costs optional (the customer chooses when to pay for new versions). We demonstrate that the Δ model is not only more sovereign but more economical — the vendor captures equivalent revenue through higher per-unit margins, and the customer saves 60-80% over 5 years compared to equivalent SaaS products.

## Why The Anticloud

The cloud industry has trained an entire generation of buyers to accept that software is an operating expense — something you rent month to month forever. This is not the natural order of computing. It is a business model that was bolted onto an architecture that required it. The Anticloud does not require it. Our software runs on your hardware, with no dependency on our infrastructure, and the price reflects that independence. You buy it once. You own it forever. You choose when to update. That is the economics of sovereignty.

ΔaaS requires one machine, one binary, and zero trust in anyone.
