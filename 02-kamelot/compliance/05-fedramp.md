## Overview

This document describes Kamelot's readiness for FedRAMP (Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program) compliance. FedRAMP provides a standardized approach to security assessment, authorization, and continuous monitoring for cloud products and services used by U.S. federal agencies.

### FedRAMP Approach

Kamelot is designed to support FedRAMP authorization at the Moderate impact level. The system implements security controls from NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 that are mapped to FedRAMP baseline requirements.

### Deployment Models for FedRAMP

Kamelot can be deployed in the following configurations for FedRAMP:

- **IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service)**: Kamelot deployed on FedRAMP-authorized IaaS (AWS GovCloud, Azure Government, GCP Assured Workloads). The IaaS provider handles physical and environmental controls.
- **On-Premise**: Kamelot deployed on federal agency-controlled infrastructure. The agency handles all physical and environmental controls.
- **Hybrid**: Kamelot deployed across FedRAMP-authorized IaaS with agency-controlled identity management.

## Impact Level Categorization

FIPS 199 requires federal information systems to be categorized as Low, Moderate, or High impact based on the potential impact of a security breach.

### Kamelot Impact Levels

| Impact Level | FIPS 199 Criteria | Kamelot Readiness |
|--------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Low | Limited adverse effect | Ready |
| Moderate | Serious adverse effect | Ready with configuration |
| High | Severe/catastrophic adverse effect | Partial (additional controls needed) |

### Low Impact Baseline

For Low impact systems, Kamelot provides baseline security controls including: basic access controls, audit logging, identification and authentication, system integrity monitoring, and basic contingency planning.

### Moderate Impact Baseline

For Moderate impact systems (the target baseline), Kamelot provides all required controls including: multi-factor authentication, encrypted communications, intrusion detection, comprehensive audit trails, disaster recovery, and continuous monitoring.

### High Impact Baseline

For High impact systems, Kamelot requires additional configuration and complementary controls including: hardware security modules, dedicated security appliances, air-gapped deployment, and enhanced continuous monitoring with real-time alerting.

## FedRAMP Authorization Path

### Authorization Pathway and Timeline

The FedRAMP authorization process follows a structured pathway with clearly defined phases, each requiring specific deliverables and milestones. The complete authorization timeline typically spans 12-24 months from initiation to JAB (Joint Authorization Board) Provisional Authorization or agency Authorization to Operate (ATO). Phase 1 (Preparation, 2-4 months) involves selecting a 3PAO (Third-Party Assessment Organization), developing the System Security Plan (SSP) core documentation, and conducting a readiness assessment. Phase 2 (Documentation, 3-6 months) requires completing the full SSP package including policies, procedures, architectural diagrams, data flow diagrams, incident response plans, and contingency plans. Phase 3 (Assessment, 3-6 months) includes the 3PAO conducting a full security assessment against NIST SP 800-53 controls (baseline of 325 controls for High impact level), penetration testing, vulnerability scanning, and compliance validation. Phase 4 (Authorization, 2-4 months) involves the JAB or agency reviewing the 3PAO assessment report, SSP, and supporting evidence before issuing a Provisional Authorization (P-ATO) or agency ATO. Phase 5 (Continuous Monitoring, ongoing) requires monthly vulnerability scans, annual control assessments, incident reporting, and Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) management. For Kamelot's self-hosted architecture, the FedRAMP authorization pathway differs from cloud services. Kamelot operating in a self-hosted configuration means the CSP (Cloud Service Provider) role effectively belongs to the deploying organization. Kamelot provides FedRAMP-authorized reference architecture, pre-configured SSP templates, and compliance automation tooling, but the authorization itself must be pursued by the deploying organization or through a FedRAMP-authorized managed service provider.

### Cost Estimates and Resource Requirements

FedRAMP authorization costs vary significantly based on impact level, system complexity, and existing compliance infrastructure. Industry estimates for initial authorization costs range from $500,000 to $2,000,000 for Low impact level systems, $1,000,000 to $3,000,000 for Moderate impact level systems, and $2,000,000 to $5,000,000 for High impact level systems. These costs include 3PAO assessment fees ($150,000-$500,000 depending on controls scope and system complexity), SSP development and documentation ($100,000-$300,000 for consulting support), penetration testing ($50,000-$150,000 for full-scope assessment), tooling and infrastructure ($100,000-$500,000 for compliance automation, continuous monitoring, and security tooling), and internal team resources (2-4 FTE for 12-24 months, representing $200,000-$800,000 in labor costs). Continuous monitoring costs add $100,000-$300,000 annually for ongoing 3PAO assessments, vulnerability scanning, POA&M management, and compliance reporting. For self-hosted software providers like Kamelot, the cost structure differs because the authorization applies to the software product rather than a specific deployment environment. Kamelot's FedRAMP strategy focuses on providing pre-configured, compliance-ready components that reduce the authorization burden for deploying organizations: pre-audited baseline configurations, automated SSP generation from deployment manifests, built-in audit logging with FedRAMP-required controls, and continuous monitoring dashboards that satisfy POA&M reporting requirements. This approach shifts the cost from upfront authorization to ongoing compliance verification, reducing the total cost of ownership for FedRAMP-authorized file management infrastructure.

### 3PAO Selection and Continuous Monitoring Requirements

Selecting a FedRAMP-accredited 3PAO is a critical decision that impacts authorization timeline, assessment quality, and long-term compliance relationship. The FedRAMP PMO maintains a list of accredited 3PAOs on the FedRAMP marketplace, with approximately 30 organizations currently holding accreditation. Key selection criteria include FedRAMP experience (number of completed authorizations, particularly at the target impact level), technical expertise in the software category (file storage, search, synchronization), assessment methodology (automated testing approach, evidence collection processes), and capacity and availability (current workload, expected timeline). Leading 3PAOs include Coalfire, Schellman, A-LIGN, EY, and KPMG, with assessment fees varying based on scope and complexity. The 3PAO relationship extends beyond initial authorization into continuous monitoring. FedRAMP requires monthly vulnerability scans with results submitted to the FedRAMP PMO within 30 days, annual control testing and assessment (a subset of controls tested in depth, with all controls tested over a 3-year period), significant change requests for any system modifications affecting security posture, incident reporting within 1 hour for confirmed incidents affecting federal data, and annual POA&M reviews with monthly status updates. For self-hosted deployments, continuous monitoring responsibility shifts from the CSP to the deploying organization. Kamelot's compliance automation tooling addresses this shift by providing automated control evidence collection, continuous compliance dashboards for real-time control status, vulnerability scanning integration with FedRAMP-accepted scanners (Nessus, Qualys, Rapid7), and automated POA&M generation with remediation tracking. These capabilities reduce the continuous monitoring burden while ensuring that self-hosted deployments maintain continuous compliance with FedRAMP requirements.
