# Governance Model

## 1. Overview

Libern is an open-source project governed by a transparent, community-driven
model that balances the needs of users, contributors, and the broader
ecosystem. The governance model is designed to ensure the long-term health,
sustainability, and integrity of the project while maintaining the core
principles of sovereignty, privacy, and decentralization that define Libern.

This document defines:
1. The project's governance structure
2. Roles and responsibilities
3. Decision-making processes
4. How the community participates in governance
5. How conflicts are resolved

## 2. Governance Philosophy

### 2.1 Core Principles

Libern's governance is guided by these principles:

- **Transparency:** All decisions, discussions, and processes are public and
  documented
- **Meritocracy:** Influence is earned through contribution quality, not
  corporate affiliation or financial contribution
- **Decentralization:** Decision-making authority is distributed to prevent
  capture by any single entity
- **Stability:** The project prioritizes long-term health over short-term
  gains
- **Inclusivity:** Anyone can participate regardless of background, location,
  or affiliation
- **Accountability:** Leaders are accountable to the community and can be
  removed through established processes

### 2.2 Project Identity

Libern is developed under the Lois-Kleinner and 0-1.gg umbrella. The project
is released under the AGPL-3.0 license. The governance structure described here
applies to the Libern open-source project, including the core application,
documentation, and associated tools.

### 3.2 Community Participants

**Definition:** Anyone who uses Libern, files issues, participates in
discussions, or otherwise engages with the project.

**Rights:**
- Access to all public project resources (source code, documentation, issue
  tracker, discussions)
- Ability to file issues and feature requests
- Participation in community discussions and votes on community polls
- Ability to fork the project under the terms of the AGPL-3.0 license

**Responsibilities:**
- Follow the Code of Conduct
- Engage respectfully with other community members
- Provide constructive feedback

### 3.3 Contributors

**Definition:** Community participants who contribute to the project through
code, documentation, translations, design, testing, or other valuable work.

**Becoming a Contributor:**
- Submit a pull request that is accepted
- Contribute to documentation, translations, or other non-code assets
- Actively participate in issue triage or community support
- Contributions are recognized in the project's contributors list

**Rights:**
- All rights of community participants
- Labeled as a contributor in project communications
- Invitation to contributor discussions
- Early access to project announcements

**Responsibilities:**
- All responsibilities of community participants
- Follow project contribution guidelines
- Participate in code review when requested
- Maintain quality standards in contributions

### 3.4 Maintainers

**Definition:** Trusted contributors who have write access to the repository
and are responsible for reviewing contributions, managing releases, and
maintaining project quality.

**Becoming a Maintainer:**
- Sustained contribution history (typically 6+ months)
- Demonstrated technical expertise in relevant areas
- Demonstrated alignment with project values and governance
- Nominated by an existing maintainer
- Approved by the Core Team
- No specific number of maintainers is defined; the Core Team adds maintainers
  as needed

**Rights:**
- All rights of contributors
- Write access to the repository
- Ability to review and merge pull requests
- Ability to label and triage issues
- Vote in maintainer-level decisions
- Eligibility to join the Core Team

**Responsibilities:**
- All responsibilities of contributors
- Review pull requests in a timely manner
- Mentor new contributors
- Participate in maintainer discussions
- Uphold project quality and governance standards
- Recuse from decisions where they have a conflict of interest

**Removal:**
- Inactive for 12+ months may be moved to emeritus status
- Violation of Code of Conduct
- Abuse of maintainer privileges
- Removal requires Core Team consensus

### 3.5 Core Team

**Definition:** A subset of maintainers who guide the project's technical
direction, manage the governance process, and make decisions that require
broader consensus.

**Composition:**
- 5-9 members
- Diverse expertise across the project's technical areas
- Representatives from different organizations and backgrounds (no single
  entity may hold a majority)

**Becoming a Core Team Member:**
- Must be a maintainer in good standing
- Nominated by an existing Core Team member
- Demonstrated leadership in the project
- Approved by consensus of the Core Team
- Final confirmation by the Steering Committee

**Rights:**
- All rights of maintainers
- Vote on Core Team decisions
- Access to private security discussions
- Participate in strategic planning
- Represent the project in external communications

**Responsibilities:**
- All responsibilities of maintainers
- Guide project technical direction
- Manage the contribution pipeline
- Handle Code of Conduct enforcement
- Oversee the release process
- Manage project resources (CI, domains, etc.)

### 3.6 Steering Committee

**Definition:** The ultimate decision-making body for the project, responsible
for strategic direction, conflict resolution, and protecting the project's
long-term interests.

**Composition:**
- 3-5 members
- Includes representatives from Lois-Kleinner and 0-1.gg
- Includes community-elected representatives
- No single entity may hold more than 2 seats

**Becoming a Steering Committee Member:**
- Appointment by Lois-Kleinner (1-2 seats)
- Appointment by 0-1.gg (1 seat)
- Election by the Core Team (1-2 seats)
- Term: 2 years, renewable

**Rights:**
- Final decision on project strategic direction
- Veto power on decisions that fundamentally alter the project's mission
- Authority to amend the governance model
- Appointment and removal of Core Team members (with cause)

**Responsibilities:**
- Protect the project's core values and mission
- Resolve escalated disputes
- Approve major strategic changes
- Oversee the project's legal and financial matters
- Ensure the project remains healthy and sustainable

## 4. Decision-Making Processes

### 4.1 Decision Types

| Decision Type | Decision Maker | Process |
|---------------|---------------|---------|
| Bug fix | Individual maintainer | Review and merge |
| Feature addition | Core Team | RFC + consensus |
| API change | Core Team | RFC + consensus |
| Release schedule | Core Team | Consensus |
| Governance change | Steering Committee | Proposal + vote |
| License change | Steering Committee | Community vote |
| New maintainer | Core Team | Consensus |
| Code of Conduct enforcement | Core Team | Consensus |

### 4.2 Consensus-Based Decision Making

Most project decisions use a consensus-based process:

1. **Propose:** An issue or discussion thread is opened
2. **Discuss:** Community and maintainers discuss the proposal (minimum 7 days)
3. **Refine:** The proposal is refined based on feedback
4. **Decide:** Maintainers/Core Team indicate their position
5. **Consensus Check:** If no blocking objections remain, consensus is reached
6. **Document:** The decision is recorded

Consensus does not mean unanimous agreement Гўв‚¬вЂќ it means that no participant has
a blocking objection that has not been addressed.

### 4.3 Voting

When consensus cannot be reached:

- **Maintainer votes:** Simple majority
- **Core Team votes:** Supermajority (2/3)
- **Steering Committee votes:** Simple majority
- **Community votes:** Defined per proposal

Voting periods are typically 7-14 days.

### 4.4 Request for Comments (RFC) Process

Significant changes use the RFC process:

1. **Pre-RFC:** Discussion in the community to gauge interest
2. **RFC Draft:** Formal document describing the proposal
3. **RFC Review:** Minimum 2-week review period with structured feedback
4. **RFC Decision:** Core Team decides to accept, reject, or request changes
5. **RFC Implementation:** Accepted RFC is implemented
6. **RFC Tracking:** Implemented RFCs are tracked in a registry

## 6. Conflict Resolution

### 6.1 Escalation Path

1. **Direct resolution:** Parties attempt to resolve the conflict directly
2. **Maintainer mediation:** A maintainer mediates the discussion
3. **Core Team review:** Core Team reviews and makes a decision
4. **Steering Committee appeal:** Final appeal if Core Team decision is
   contested

### 6.2 Code of Conduct Violations

Code of Conduct violations are handled separately through the enforcement
process (see code-of-conduct.md). The Core Team is responsible for
investigation and enforcement.

### 6.3 Technical Disputes

Technical disputes follow the RFC process:
1. Both sides present their arguments in a structured format
2. Technical merits are evaluated, not authority or seniority
3. Decisions are based on project values, not personal preference
4. Minority opinions are documented for future reference

## 8. Governance Evolution

### 8.1 Amendments

The governance model may be amended by:
1. Proposal to the Steering Committee
2. 2-week public comment period
3. Steering Committee vote (simple majority)
4. If the amendment changes the governance model fundamentally, a community
   vote may be required

### 8.2 Periodic Review

The governance model is reviewed annually by the Steering Committee and Core
Team to ensure it continues to serve the project's needs.

## 9. Transparency and Reporting

### 9.1 Public Records

All governance decisions are documented in the project repository:
- RFC decisions
- Core Team meeting notes (public)
- Steering Committee meeting notes (public, with sensitive redaction)
- Maintainer decisions

### 9.2 Annual Report

The Core Team publishes an annual report covering:
- Project activity and growth
- Financial transparency
- Governance changes
- Community health metrics
- Roadmap and priorities

**Resolution:**
1. Both sides write up their technical arguments with code examples
2. A third maintainer reviews both approaches
3. A test suite is created to compare correctness and performance
4. The Core Team votes based on objective test results
5. The decision and minority opinion are documented

### 13.2 Voting Procedures

| Decision Type | Voters | Threshold | Voting Period | Quorum |
|--------------|--------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| RFC acceptance | Core Team | Consensus (>2/3) | 14 days | 2/3 of members |
| New maintainer | Core Team | Consensus (>2/3) | 7 days | Majority |
| Core Team member | Core Team + Steering | Consensus (>2/3) | 14 days | 2/3 of each |
| Steering member | Core Team election | Majority | 14 days | Majority |
| Governance change | Steering + community | Steering majority | 21 days | All steering |
| License change | All contributors | Supermajority (3/4) | 30 days | N/A (all votes counted) |
| Code of Conduct action | Core Team | Consensus | 7 days | 2/3 of members |

### 13.3 Recusal Policy

Members must recuse themselves from votes where they have:
- Financial interest in the outcome
- Personal relationship with a nominee
- Direct competition with a proposed solution
- Other conflicts per the Code of Conduct

## 10. Conclusion

Libern's governance model is designed to be:
- **Open:** Anyone can participate
- **Meritocratic:** Influence follows contribution
- **Accountable:** Leaders are answerable to the community
- **Sustainable:** The model ensures long-term project health
- **Transparent:** Decisions are documented and explainable

This model may evolve as the project grows. Community feedback on the
governance model itself is always welcome.
